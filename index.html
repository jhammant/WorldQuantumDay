You Already Know This

A classical bit is 0 or 1. A NOT gate flips it. AND, OR, and XOR combine pairs of bits. From these primitives, you build everything — processors, operating systems, the internet.

What Changes

A qubit can be 0, 1, or a superposition of both simultaneously. This isn't "maybe 0 or maybe 1 and we don't know." The qubit genuinely exists in both states at once, weighted by complex numbers called amplitudes.

Think of a coin: a classical bit is heads or tails on the table. A qubit is a coin spinning in the air — it has some probability of landing each way, but while spinning, it's in a state that is neither purely heads nor tails.

The coin analogy breaks down because quantum amplitudes can be negative and complex, enabling interference. That's where the real power comes from.

The Three Big Ideas

Superposition: A qubit processes multiple possibilities at once. N qubits can represent 2ⁿ states simultaneously.

Entanglement: Correlations between qubits that have no classical equivalent. Measuring one instantly determines the other, no matter the distance.

Interference: Amplitudes can cancel (destructive) or reinforce (constructive). Quantum algorithms use this to amplify right answers and suppress wrong ones.

Regular Computers

A regular computer uses bits — tiny switches that are either OFF (0) or ON (1). Everything your computer does is built from billions of these switches.

Quantum Computers Are Different

A quantum computer uses qubits. The magic? A qubit can be both 0 and 1 at the same time — like a coin spinning in the air before it lands.

This lets quantum computers explore many answers simultaneously instead of checking them one by one.

Three Superpowers

Superposition: Try all answers at once (like reading every book in a library simultaneously).

Entanglement: Link qubits so they instantly affect each other (Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance").

Interference: Make wrong answers cancel out and right answers get louder (like noise-canceling headphones for computation).

Reading the Bloch Sphere

The sphere on screen is a map of every possible state of a single qubit.

North pole = |0⟩ (definitely 0, like a classical bit set to 0)

South pole = |1⟩ (definitely 1)

Equator = equal superposition — 50/50 between 0 and 1

Everywhere else = unequal superposition. Closer to a pole means more likely to measure that value.

What's Phase?

Phase is the angle around the equator. It doesn't affect the probability of measuring 0 vs 1, but it determines how the qubit interferes with other qubits — like two waves being in sync or out of sync.

Try: Apply H twice. Why does it return to |0⟩?
H creates superposition, and H undoes it. Applying H twice is the identity — it's its own inverse. Mathematically: H×H = I.
Try: H → Z → H. This equals X! Why?
H transforms between the Z-basis and X-basis. "Sandwiching" Z between two H gates converts a phase flip into a bit flip. This is called change of basis.
Try: H then X. Where does the arrow point?
It points to |−⟩ (the negative X-axis). H creates |+⟩, then X flips it to |−⟩. The probabilities are still 50/50, but the phase changed.

What Makes a Quantum Algorithm

Step 1: Put qubits in superposition (H gates)

Step 2: Apply problem-specific gates that create interference

Step 3: Measure to read the answer

The demos in the next tabs follow exactly this pattern.

The Problem: Find one specific item in an unsorted database of N items.
Classical: Check one by one. Average N/2 steps.
Grover's: Superposition + amplitude amplification. ~√N steps.
Use cases: database search, SAT solving, optimization subroutines
Classical: 0/256 Grover: 0/13
The Problem: Factor a large number into primes. This is the foundation of RSA encryption — HTTPS, banking, digital signatures.
Classical: Trial division. For RSA-2048: ~300 trillion years.
Shor's: Quantum Fourier transform finds hidden periodicity. Hours, not eons.
Impact: NIST standardized post-quantum cryptography in 2024 because of this algorithm
Classical: 0 divisors tested Quantum: 0 iterations
The Problem: Find the shortest route visiting all cities (Traveling Salesman). 8 cities = 40,320 possible routes.
Classical: Try routes one by one, or use heuristics (good but not guaranteed optimal).
Quantum QAOA: Encode all routes in superposition, amplify shorter routes through variational optimization.
Use cases: logistics (Amazon, FedEx), financial portfolios, drug design, chip routing, airline scheduling
Paths tested: 0/40,320 QAOA iteration: 0/30
The Problem: Share a secret key when someone might be eavesdropping.
Classical: You can't detect eavesdropping. If Eve copies your messages, you'll never know.
Quantum BB84: Measuring a quantum state disturbs it. Eavesdropping introduces detectable errors.
Already deployed: China's quantum satellite link, European QKD networks, Swiss banking
Classical: Eve undetected Quantum: 0 photons sent

Run a Quantum Computer TODAY

IBM lets anyone run real quantum circuits for free. Here's a Qiskit program that creates superposition and measures it — on actual quantum hardware:

from qiskit import QuantumCircuit from qiskit_ibm_runtime import SamplerV2 # Create a circuit: H gate then measure qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1) qc.h(0) # Superposition! qc.measure(0, 0) # Run on real quantum hardware sampler = SamplerV2() result = sampler.run([qc]).result() print(result[0].data.c.get_counts()) # {'0': ~500, '1': ~500}
